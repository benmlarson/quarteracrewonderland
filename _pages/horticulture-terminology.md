### Deadheading

**Deadheading** is the practice of removing spent (dead or fading) flowers from a plant. By removing the spent bloom, you're telling the plant to keep producing flowers instead of redirecting energy into seed production.

**Benefits:**
- More blooms — the plant keeps flowering instead of going to seed
- Longer bloom season — extends flowering by weeks or even months
- Tidier appearance — no brown, wilted flowers hanging around
- Prevents unwanted self-seeding

**How to do it:** Pinch or snip the stem just above the next set of leaves or a leaf node. Use your fingers for soft stems and pruners for woodier ones.

**When not to deadhead:** Some plants have attractive seed heads worth keeping — coneflowers and ornamental grasses add winter interest and feed birds.

### Hardening Off

**Hardening off** is the process of gradually acclimating indoor-grown seedlings to outdoor conditions — sunlight, wind, temperature swings, and lower humidity — before transplanting them into the garden.

**Why it matters:** Seedlings grown indoors have never felt wind, direct sun, or nighttime temperature drops. Moving them outside abruptly can cause sunscald, wilting, or transplant shock.

**How to do it (7-10 days):**
1. **Day 1-2** — set seedlings outside in a shady, sheltered spot for 2-3 hours
2. **Day 3-4** — increase to 4-5 hours, introducing some morning sun
3. **Day 5-7** — move to a sunnier spot for most of the day
4. **Day 8-10** — leave them out all day and overnight (if no frost threat)
5. **Transplant** into the garden

**Common mistakes:** Putting seedlings in full sun on day one, forgetting to water small pots that dry out fast in wind, or skipping the process entirely.

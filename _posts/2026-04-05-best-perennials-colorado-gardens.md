March's plant of the month is lavender — a drought-tolerant perennial that's perfectly suited to the Western Slope's dry climate and alkaline soil.

## Why Lavender?

It's fragrant, beautiful, low-maintenance, and the pollinators absolutely love it. A few lavender plants along a walkway or border will perfume your entire garden from June through August.

## Best Varieties for the Western Slope

- **Munstead** — compact English lavender, cold-hardy to Zone 5
- **Hidcote** — deep purple flowers, slightly more compact than Munstead
- **Phenomenal** — bred for heat and humidity tolerance, very reliable

## Growing Tips

Lavender wants full sun and well-drained soil. In our heavy clay, amend the planting area with gravel or coarse sand to improve drainage — wet feet in winter is the number one killer of lavender in Colorado.

- **Don't overwater** — once established, lavender thrives on neglect
- **Prune in spring** — cut back about a third of the plant after new growth appears
- **Skip the fertilizer** — rich soil actually reduces fragrance and oil production

## Harvest and Use

Cut stems just as the flowers begin to open for the strongest fragrance. Bundle and hang upside down to dry. Dried lavender is wonderful in sachets, baked goods, and homemade soap.
